# Longchen Rabjampa Drime Özer

## ཀློང་ཆེན་རབ་འབྱམས་དྲི་མེད་འོད་ཟེར།

### The Omniscient One of the Great Expanse

---

## Name and Titles

**Longchen Rabjampa** (ཀློང་ཆེན་རབ་འབྱམས་), "Longchenpa," literally means "One Who Has Vast Expanse of Knowledge" or "Greatly Learned One of the Expanse." His full name, **Drime Özer** (དྲིེད་འོད་མ་ཟེར་), translates to "Stainless Light Ray"—the name of a great treasure revealer (terton).

He is also known by the honorific title **Kunkhyen** (ཀུན་མཁྱེན་), meaning "The Omniscient One," acknowledging his unrivaled scholarly achievements in Tibetan Buddhism.

**Wylie Transliteration:** klong chen rab 'byams / dri med 'od zer

**IAST Transliteration:** Kloṃ chen rab 'byams / Drime Özer

---

## Birth and Origins

**Born:** 1308 CE  
**Died:** 1364 CE  
**Birthplace:** G.yo ru (Yoru), Central Tibet  
**Tibetan Calendar:** Earth Monkey, Rabjung 5  
**Gender:** Male

Longchen Rabjampa was born in the central region of G.yo ru (Yoru), situated in the area known as Dokham (Eastern Tibet). According to traditional Nyingma biographical accounts, he was recognized as the direct reincarnation (sprul sku) of several important figures:

1. **Padma las 'brel rtsal** — The great Indian master who revealed the *Rdzogs chen snying thig* (Great Completeness Heart Essence) teachings
2. **Padma gsal** — The original recipient of these teachings from Padmasambhava (Guru Rinpoche)
3. **Vimalamitra** — The Indian scholar who transmitted the Dzogchen teachings to Tibet

This lineage of emanations places Longchen Rabjampa at the heart of the Nyingma Great Perfection tradition, establishing him as a key figure in the transmission of Dzogchen (rdzogs chen) teachings.

---

## Early Education and Monastic Life

### Ordination

At the age of twelve, young Longchen received monastic ordination, entering the Buddhist monastic discipline of the Nyingma tradition. This early ordination was typical for promising young scholars in medieval Tibet, where education and religious training began in childhood.

### Advanced Studies at Gsang Phu Ne'u Thog

At nineteen, Longchen Rabjampa entered the renowned **Gsang phu ne'u thog** monastery—one of the most important academic centers in medieval Tibet. Located near Lhasa, this monastery was famed for its rigorous curriculum in Buddhist philosophy, scholastic debate, and the traditional sciences.

At Gsang phu ne'u thog, he engaged in extensive studies including:

- **Buddhist Philosophy** — The elaborate systems of logic, epistemology, and metaphysical debate
- **Sūtra Literature** — The collection of Buddha's discourses
- **Tantric Systems** — The three classes of tantra: Kriyā yoga, Ubhaya yoga (yogāntara), and Mahāyoga/Anuttara yoga
- **Traditional Buddhist Sciences** — Grammar (sgra), poetics (snyan ngag), medicine (sman), and metrics

### Teachers and Influences

Longchen Rabjampa trained under masters representing the breadth of Tibetan Buddhist traditions:

- **The Abbots of Gsang phu ne'u thog** — Representing the scholarly mainstream of Tibetan Buddhism
- **The Third Karmapa, Rang 'byung rdo rje** (1284-1339) — One of the greatest Kagyu masters of the period
- **G.yag sde paN chen** — A leading scholar of the time
- **Bsod nams rgyal mtshan** — Prominent Sa skya teacher
- **Kyitön Drakpa Gyaltsen** — Another influential scholar

This cross-traditional training gave Longchen Rabjampa an unprecedented mastery of multiple Buddhist lineages, including the **Rnying ma** (Old Translation), **Sa skya**, and **Bka' brgyud** (Kagyu) schools.

---

## Disillusionment and Wandering Ascetic

Despite his extraordinary scholarly achievements, Longchen Rabjampa became disillusioned with the academic monastic environment of his time. According to biographical sources, he grew troubled by the arrogance and pretension he observed among many scholars—monks more concerned with intellectual status than genuine realization.

In his **mid-twenties**, Longchen made the radical decision to leave the monastic life and pursue the path of a wandering ascetic (*yang dro*). This choice reflected his deep conviction that authentic spiritual realization could not be attained through scholarly debate alone, but required direct meditative experience and lived wisdom.

This period of wandering took him across Tibet, where he would have encountered various masters, practitioners, and local traditions—experiences that would later inform his writings.

---

## Meeting Kumārarāja and Dzogchen Transmission

At the age of twenty-nine, a pivotal encounter changed the course of Longchen Rabjampa's life and legacy. At the ancient **Bsam yas** monastery—the first Buddhist temple in Tibet, founded by King Trisong Detsen in the eighth century—he met the great yogin **Kumārarāja** (Rig 'dzin ku mA ra rA dza).

Kumārarāja accepted Longchen as his disciple and transmitted to him the **three classes of Dzogchen** (*rdzogs chen sde gsum*):

1. **Mind Series** (*sems sde*) — The teachings on the nature of mind
2. **Space Series** (*klong sde*) — The teachings on the expanse of reality
3. **Instruction Series** (*man ngag sde*) — Direct pith instructions for realization

This transmission became the foundation of Longchen Rabjampa's subsequent teaching career and the basis for his monumental literary output. The Dzogchen teachings, particularly the *snying thig* (Heart Essence) tradition, would become the central focus of his scholarly and contemplative life.

---

## Hermitage at Gangri Thökar

Following his Dzogchen initiation, Longchen Rabjampa spent much of his remaining life in retreat, primarily at the hermitage of **Gangs ri thod dkar** (Gangri Thökar), a remote meditation site in the mountains of Tibet.

It was at Gangri Thökar that he composed most of his important works, particularly his redaction of the meditation and ritual manuals of the **Snying thig** (Heart Essence) tradition. The peace and solitude of this hermitage allowed him to integrate his vast scholarly knowledge with direct meditative realization.

---

## Major Literary Works

Longchen Rabjampa's literary corpus is one of the most extensive in Tibetan Buddhist history. His works number in the dozens, spanning thousands of volumes. Among the most important:

### The Seven Treasuries (Mdzod bdun)

The **Seven Treasuries** (མཛོད་བདུན་) is his magnum opus—a comprehensive systematic presentation of Buddhist philosophy, practice, and Dzogchen theory:

1. **Kun gzhi mdzod** (Treasury of the All-Base) — On the ground of being
2. **Ye shes mdzod** (Treasury of Wisdom) — On pristine cognition
3. **Sku mdzod** (Treasury of the Body) — On the three bodies of a buddha
4. **Snying rtags mdzod** (Treasury of the Heart Essence) — On Dzogchen
5. **Chos mdzod** (Treasury of Dharma) — On doctrine
6. **Mdo mdzod** (Treasury of the Sūtras) — On scriptural collections
7. **Man ngag mdzod** (Treasury of Instructions) — On practice

### Trilogy on Rest (Ngal gso skor gsum)

A more accessible trilogy for practitioners:

- **Gang jung ngal gso** (Resting in the Nature of Mind)
- **Ngal gso skor gsum** (Threefold Refuge)
- **Rdo rje sems dpa' ngal gso** (Vajrasattva Resting)

### Heart Essence (Snying thig) Works

Longchen Rabjampa's redaction and exposition of the *Snying thig* (Heart Essence) teachings became definitive for the Nyingma tradition. These include:

- **Snying thig ya bshad** (Great Commentary on the Heart Essence)
- Various sadhana (practice) texts and ritual manuals

### Other Major Works

- **Dris lan** (Dispelling Darkness) — A response to critiques of Dzogchen
- **Shing rta chen po** (The Great Chariot)
- Numerous commentaries on sutras, tantras, and Dzogchen texts
- Poetry and devotional works

## Philosophical Positions

Longchen Rabjampa's philosophical contributions are profound and nuanced:

### Buddha-Nature

He maintained that **buddha-nature** (tathāgatagarbha) must be understood as **definitive meaning** (*nges don*), not merely provisional. His interpretation aligns with the Third Turning of the Dharma wheel, emphasizing that buddha-nature is not simply emptiness (śūnyatā) but luminous clarity—**the unity of emptiness and awareness**.

### Emptiness and Luminosity

Longchen Rabjampa presented what scholars call **"emptiness as luminosity"**—a positive ontology where ultimate reality is not mere negation but vivid, self-presenting awareness. He drew on the *Ratnagotravibhāga* (Uttaratantra) to support this view.

### Zhentong (Empty of Other)

While he sparingly used the term **zhentong** (_EMPTY of Other_), his presentations differ from how it would later be characterized by Dolpopa Sherab Gyaltsen. He maintained that all beings possess buddha-nature, though it is obscured by adventitious stains (*yon tan*), and this luminosity is present even within samsara.

### Madhyamaka Position

His presentation of emptiness clearly favors the **Prāsaṃgika** (Consequence) school, emphasizing that phenomena lack inherent existence while avoiding any assertion of positive ontological claims. However, his Dzogchen presentations go beyond even Madhyamaka to assert the "ground of being" (*gzhi*) as the basis for both samsara and nirvana.

## Students and Legacy

Longchen Rabjampa transmitted his knowledge to numerous students, continuing the Dzogchen lineage:

- **Rig 'dzin Ku mA rA dza** — His root teacher, who transmitted the three classes of Dzogchen
- **Third Karmapa Rangjung Dorje** — The great Kagyu master who received transmissions from Longchen
- Numerous other practitioners across Tibet

### Subsequent Emanations

As a recognized terton (treasure revealer), Longchen Rabjampa was considered to have emanations in subsequent generations:

- **Dudjom Terse Drime Özer** (1904-1987) — The great Nyingma master who revealed the Dudjom Tersar
- **Lhatsun Namkha Jikme** — Another important terton
- **Pema Lingpa** (1450-1521) — Famous Bhutanese terton
- **Pema Lodrö** — Later incarnation

## Significance and Influence

Longchen Rabjampa stands as one of the most influential figures in Tibetan Buddhist history:

### Scholarly Achievement

His ** Seven Treasuries** remains one of the most comprehensive systematic presentations of Buddhist philosophy and practice ever produced in Tibet. His writings demonstrate mastery of:

- All major Buddhist philosophical systems
- The entire range of sutra and tantra literature
- The specialized fields of grammar, poetics, and metrics

### Unifying Tradition

His cross-traditional training allowed him to synthesize teachings from the Nyingma, Sa skya, and Kagyu schools, creating a unified presentation of Buddhist doctrine that transcended sectarian boundaries.

### Practice Integration

Unlike purely academic scholars, Longchen Rabjampa integrated scholarly understanding with genuine contemplative achievement. His works are valued not only as philosophical texts but as guides to direct realization.

### Foundational for Nyingma

His redaction of the Snying thig (Heart Essence) materials became the standard version used by the Nyingma tradition. Practically all later Nyingma practitioners, including the great seventeenth-century tertons, drew on his work.

---

## Conclusion

Longchen Rabjampa Drime Özer (1308-1364) represents the pinnacle of Tibetan Buddhist scholarship combined with authentic Dzogchen realization. His life journey—from prodigy monk to disillusioned academic to wandering ascetic to realized master—reflects the Buddhist emphasis on wisdom beyond mere intellectual knowledge.

His literary legacy, particularly the **Seven Treasuries** and his Heart Essence works, continues to serve as foundational texts for practitioners and scholars of Dzogchen worldwide. As the "Omniscient One of the Great Expanse," he embodies the ideal of one who has traversed the entire range of Buddhist learning and arrived at direct realization of the nature of mind—the ultimate "treasure" he revealed for all subsequent generations.
