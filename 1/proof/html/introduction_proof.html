VOLUME 1: The Ground Manifest

Cosmology, Philosophy, and the Subtle Body

Tibetan: ཐེག་པའི་མཆོག་རིན་པོ་ཆེའི་མཛོད་ཀྱི་འགྲེལ་པ། (Theg mchog rin po che'i mdzod kyi 'grel pa)
Full Title: Treasury of the Supreme Vehicle: Autocommentary
Chapter Range: Chapters 1-14 (The Former Volume - snga ma)
Sections: 106
Approximate Lines: 20,426
Author: Longchen Rabjampa (1308-1364)
Translation Period: 2024-2026

Overview

This first volume establishes the complete framework within which the Great Perfection unfolds. Beginning with pure vision and descending through cosmological description, philosophical analysis, and subtle body physiology, these fourteen chapters provide the ground (gzhi) upon which all subsequent realization rests.

Longchenpa's genius lies in his systematic presentation: rather than plunging immediately into advanced Dzogchen practices, he first establishes the total context of Buddhist thought and cosmology. Volume 1 answers: What is the nature of reality? How do beings arise and dwell in cyclic existence? What is the philosophical basis for liberation? How does the subtle body function as interface between mind and world?

By Chapter 14's end, the reader possesses a comprehensive map of both saṃsāra and nirvāṇa—from Akanisṭha to the most subtle channels and winds.

The Architecture of Volume 1

Chapters 1-2: Cosmological Framework - Chapter 1 establishes the pure vision of enlightenment - Chapter 2 maps the container world and contents beings

Chapters 3-5: Philosophical Foundation
- Chapter 3 presents Abhidharma phenomenology - Chapter 4 critically examines all philosophical systems - Chapter 5 establishes the tantric framework

Chapters 6-7: Vajrayāna Context - Chapter 6 presents empowerments and samaya - Chapter 7 details specific Dzogchen commitments

Chapters 8-10: The Ground Explored - Chapter 8 establishes primordial ground - Chapter 9 reveals spontaneous presence appearance - Chapter 10 presents wisdoms and kāyas

Chapters 11-14: Subtle Body Completion - Chapters 11-12 map channels and winds - Chapter 13 presents the four lamps - Chapter 14 distinguishes mind from awareness

Volume Character

Volume 1 mirrors the creation phase (bskyed rim) of tantric practice: establishing pure vision, purifying perception, preparing the vessel for completion phase practices. Just as a skilled artisan must understand both materials and blueprint, Longchenpa ensures the practitioner understands the total field within which awakening occurs.

The volume's progression—from pure vision to subtle body—reflects the traditional pedagogy: establishing view before meditation, cosmology before completion stage. This is not mere information but gradual recognition (cig car within rim gyis), stripping away obscurations to reveal what has always been present.

The Treasury Complete

Volume 1 stands as essential foundation. Without understanding the ground established here—primordial purity, the structure of existence, the subtle body infrastructure—Volume 2's completion stage practices would remain theoretical abstractions. Longchenpa ensures the vessel is prepared before the nectar is poured.

May this volume serve as the basis of recognition for all who engage with it.

Sarva mangalam.

CHAPTER 1: The Perfect Teacher

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་དང་པོ། (First Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 1, Sections 01-01-01-01 through 01-01-03-01
Tibetan Lines: 1-583
Total Liturgical Lines: ~583

The Treasury opens with a cosmogonic prologue establishing the pure vision from which all teachings emerge. Longchenpa presents Samantabhadra—the primordial Buddha embodying the five perfections—and traces emanation through the Three Kāyas to Vajradhara, the sixth teacher manifesting in this world system. The thirty-six activities of enlightenment demonstrate that buddhahood is not passive but dynamically engaged.

This chapter provides the template for all subsequent maṇḍala visualizations and guru yoga practices. Enlightenment is established as the reference point; all limitations will be measured against this completeness.

The Five Perfections: - Perfect place: Akanisṭha, field of spontaneous appearance - Perfect teacher: Samantabhadra, self-arisen wisdom
- Perfect retinue: Five vidyādharas, hosts of the aural lineage - Perfect teaching: Dharmatā luminous-clarity, Dzogchen - Perfect time: Beyond action and effort, the primordial sphere

The Three Kāyas: - Dharmakāya: Samantabhadra in Akanisṭha—primordial purity - Saṃbhogakāya: The five families appearing through outer lights - Nirmāṇakāya: Vajradhara of complete marks, the sixth teacher

Key Contribution: Establishes enlightenment as the reference point against which all subsequent limitations and errors will be measured.

CHAPTER 2: Container World and Contents Beings

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་གཉིས་པ། (Second Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 1, Sections 01-02-01-01 through 01-02-02-02
Tibetan Lines: 584-1581
Total Liturgical Lines: ~830

Having established pure vision, Longchenpa now maps the cosmic architecture within which ordinary beings dwell. This chapter presents Buddhist cosmology in full: the thirteen Thal-ba fields surrounding Sahā world, pure realms above, hell realms below.

Detailed descriptions of the eight hot hells, eight cold hells, and lifespans in each realm provide contrast to Chapter 1's purity. The chapter establishes the cosmological framework essential for understanding how beings become trapped in cyclic existence.

The Configuration: - Six transcendent realms above and below - Our Sahā world at center - Thirteen Thal-ba fields in concentric circles - Eight hot hells and eight cold hells - Calculations of cosmic time and world-system formation

Key Contribution: Maps the full range of existence from Akanisṭha to Avīci, establishing the complete field within which liberation occurs.

CHAPTER 3: Samsaric Phenomena

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་གསུམ་པ། (Third Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 1, Sections 01-03-01-01 through 01-03-03-01
Tibetan Lines: 1582-1901
Total Liturgical Lines: ~454

This brief chapter presents the Abhidharma foundation: the five elements, five aggregates, and twelve sense-bases that form the basis of conditioned existence. Longchenpa demonstrates how ordinary experience arises through the interaction of these components.

The Framework: - Five elements ('byung ba lnga): Earth, water, fire, wind, space - Five aggregates (phung po lnga): Form, feeling, discrimination, formation, consciousness
- Twelve sense-bases (skye mched bcu gnyis): Six faculties and six objects

Crucial Distinction: Longchenpa distinguishes between contaminated (zag bcas) and uncontaminated (zag med) aggregates, foreshadowing the Dzogchen view that the nature of mind is primordially pure despite temporary defilements.

Key Contribution: Establishes the standard Buddhist phenomenology that Dzogchen transcends but does not reject.

CHAPTER 4: Philosophical Systems

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་བཞི་པ། (Fourth Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 1, Sections 01-04-01-01 through 01-04-19-01
Tibetan Lines: 1902-4171
Total Liturgical Lines: ~2,100

The longest and most philosophically dense chapter—this critical survey examines all Buddhist and non-Buddhist systems. Longchenpa systematically refutes:

  • Hīnayāna (Śrāvaka and Pratyekabuddha vehicles)
  • Mind-Only (Cittamātra) systems
  • Middle Way (Mādhyamika) approaches
  • Lower tantras (Kriyā, Caryā, Yoga)

Each refutation serves as pedagogy: by understanding each system's limitations, Dzogchen's unique profundity becomes clear. The chapter culminates in presenting Atiyoga as the supreme vehicle, introducing key concepts: primordial purity (ka dag), spontaneous presence (lhun grub), and the three aspects of the ground.

Key Contribution: Positions Dzogchen within Buddhist intellectual tradition while demonstrating its superiority.

This is the bridge between sūtra and tantra, establishing why Vajrayāna is necessary for rapid liberation.

Key Contribution: Establishes the tantric context within which Dzogchen completion stage practices operate.

CHAPTER 8: Primordial Ground

Tibetan Lines: 10472-11334
Total Liturgical Lines: ~860

This chapter establishes the ground (gzhi)—the dimension of primordial purity that precedes both saṃsāra and nirvāṇa. Longchenpa presents the sevenfold nature of the ground: essence (ngo bo), nature (rang bzhin), compassion (thugs rje), and their interdependence.

Key Topics: - Primordial purity (ka dag): Unstained from the beginning - Spontaneous presence (lhun grub): Self-manifesting display - Ground vs. basis of delusion: Recognition vs. non-recognition - Two truths: From Dzogchen perspective

The chapter dismantles reification of philosophical systems while establishing the unique Dzogchen view.

Key Contribution: Establishes the ground of Dzogchen view—the basis for all subsequent recognition.

CHAPTER 9: Spontaneous Presence Appearance

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་དགུ་པ། (Ninth Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 1, Sections 01-09-01-01 through 01-09-02-01
Tibetan Lines: 11335-12477
Total Liturgical Lines: ~1,150

This extensive chapter reveals spontaneous presence (lhun grub)—the dynamic display of the ground that manifests as both saṃsāra and nirvāṇa. Longchenpa explains how the same ground, through recognition or non-recognition, becomes either delusion or liberation.

The Display: - Spontaneous presence (lhun grub): The ground's self-manifesting luminosity - Non-recognition (ma rig pa): When presence is not recognized, it becomes delusion - Eight consciousnesses: How spontaneous presence crystallizes into ordinary mind - Twelve links: Dependent origination as spontaneous presence gone astray

The Turning Point:

"Through the door of spontaneous presence appearance, the turning point of saṃsāra and nirvāṇa is shown."

The chapter demonstrates that saṃsāra is not separate from the ground—merely the ground's display unrecognized. Liberation is not an escape but recognition of what has always been present.

Key Contribution: Reveals spontaneous presence as the bridge between ground and manifestation—essential for understanding how recognition transforms delusion into wisdom.

CHAPTER 10: Wisdoms and Kāyas

This chapter presents the fruition perspective: the five wisdoms (ye shes lnga) and three kāyas (sku gsum) as spontaneously present, not created through practice. Longchenpa demonstrates how afflictions transmute into wisdoms and how deluded aggregates become enlightened bodies.

The Five Wisdoms: 1. Dharmadhātu: Mirror-like wisdom from anger purified 2. Mirror-like: Equality wisdom from pride purified
3. Discriminating: All-accomplishing from desire purified 4. All-accomplishing: Dharmadhātu from jealousy purified 5. Equality: Discriminating from ignorance purified

The Three Kāyas: Presented as spontaneously present—not produced by causes, not destroyed by conditions.

Key Contribution: Establishes that enlightenment is the nature of mind, not something to be achieved.

CHAPTER 13: Four Lamps

CHAPTER 14: Basis of Consciousness

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་བཅུ་བཞི་པ། (Fourteenth Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 1, Sections 01-14-01-01 through 01-14-13-01
Tibetan Lines: 17361-20426
Total Liturgical Lines: ~2,925

The final chapter of Volume 1 presents the basis of mind (sems kyi gzhi)—the distinction between ordinary mind (sems) and pristine awareness (rig pa). Longchenpa analyzes the eight consciousnesses, the ālaya (kun gzhi), and how deluded perception arises from failure to recognize awareness.

Key Distinctions: - Mind (sems): Discursive, dualistic, deluded - Awareness (rig pa): Non-dual, self-recognizing, primordially pure - Ālaya (kun gzhi): Neutral basis, not afflicted, not enlightened

The Eight Consciousnesses: Eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mental consciousnesses, afflicted mind (nyon yid), and basis-consciousness (kun gzhi rnam shes).

Key Contribution: Distinguishes the Dzogchen view of awareness from Mind-Only and lower vehicle positions—essential for understanding Volume 2's practices.

VOLUME 2: Recognition and Liberation

Path, Bardo, and Fruition

Full Title: Treasury of the Supreme Vehicle: Autocommentary (Latter Volume)
Chapter Range: Chapters 15-25 (The Latter Volume - phyi ma)
Sections: 107
Approximate Lines: 17,330
Author: Longchen Rabjampa (1308-1364)
Translation Period: 2024-2026

Overview

This second volume moves from cosmological description to direct liberation. Where Volume 1 established the ground (gzhi)—pure vision, cyclic existence structure, subtle body anatomy—Volume 2 presents the path (lam) of recognition and the fruition ('bras bu) of complete awakening.

The volume's architecture mirrors the completion phase (rdzogs rim): cutting through conceptual mind (Trekchö), crossing through visionary experience (Thögal), navigating intermediate states (bardo), and ultimately dissolving all phenomena into the expanse of primordial purity. Volume 1 prepared the vessel; Volume 2 fills it with nectar of realization.

The Architecture of Volume 2

Chapters 15-16: Recognition Foundation - Chapter 15: The elements as wisdom display - Chapter 16: Expanse and wisdom nonduality

Chapters 17-18: Path Practices - Chapter 17: Systematic stages of the path
- Chapter 18: Distinguishing Dzogchen from lower vehicles

Chapters 19-20: Advanced Practices - Chapter 19: Deity visualization from Dzogchen perspective - Chapter 20: Trekchö and Thögal—the swift path

Chapters 21-24: Death and Bardo - Chapter 21: Bardo practice overview - Chapter 22: Consciousness transference (phowa) - Chapter 23: Self-appearing bardo navigation - Chapter 24: Faculties and capacities (for those of lowest ability)

Chapter 25: Spontaneous Fruition - Complete dissolution into primordial expanse - Recognition that fruition was never separate from ground - Thus the Treasury is complete.

Volume Character

Volume 2 is both prescriptive and descriptive: offering practices for those who need them while constantly pointing beyond practice to the natural state already perfect. It is the practical application of Volume 1's theoretical foundations, yet repeatedly demonstrates that theory and practice ultimately dissolve into direct recognition.

Longchenpa's pedagogical genius appears in the progression: beginning with the five elements (same as ordinary existence), demonstrating they are in essence wisdom's display. He presents progressive path instructions, advanced completion-stage practices, detailed bardo navigation, and finally reveals the Great Liberation—recognition that nothing needed doing, for fruition has always been complete.

The Fruition Complete

Volume 2 stands as practical completion. Where Volume 1 established "what is," this volume demonstrates "how to recognize what is." The bookend structure—Chapter 1's pure vision answered by Chapter 25's spontaneous fruition—reveals Longchenpa's ultimate intent: the ground has never been separate from the result.

May this volume serve as the path of recognition for all who engage with it.

May all beings realize the supreme vehicle.

Sarva mangalam.

Three Types of Phowa: 1. Dharmakāya phowa: For supreme practitioners—liberation into expanse 2. Saṃbhogakāya phowa: For middling—transference to pure field
3. Nirmāṇakāya phowa: For ordinary—rebirth as human with Dharma

Signs of Success: Specific physical signs indicating consciousness has left through crown aperture: body warmth, complexion, posture.

Key Contribution: The "insurance policy" for practitioners—ensuring favorable circumstances if recognition is not complete.

CHAPTER 23: Self-Appearing Bardo

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་ཉི་ཤུ་རྩ་གསུམ་པ། (Twenty-third Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 2, Sections 02-23-01-01 through 02-23-09-01
Tibetan Lines: —
Total Liturgical Lines: ~2,800

The most extensive bardo chapter—this detailed navigation guide describes the visions and experiences of the intermediate state. Longchenpa maps the progressive appearances: from initial luminosity through the hundred peaceful and wrathful deities to the judgment visions that determine rebirth.

The Progressive Visions: - First three days: Luminosity of dharmatā appears - Days 4-7: Peaceful deities manifest from heart centers - Days 8-14: Wrathful deities manifest from brain centers
- Days 15-49: Judgment scenes, karmic winds, seeking rebirth

Recognition vs. Delusion: For trained practitioners, these are self-appearances of awareness (rig pa rang snang). For ordinary beings, terrifying external threats.

Key Contribution: Complete map of the bardo experience—essential for practitioners preparing for death.

CHAPTER 24: Faculties and Capacities

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་ཉི་ཤུ་རྩ་བཞི་པ། (Twenty-fourth Lecture Hall)
Location: Volume 2, Sections 02-24-01-01
Tibetan Lines: —
Total Liturgical Lines: ~360

This chapter addresses practitioners of lowest faculties who cannot recognize in bardo. Longchenpa explains the process of taking rebirth and how to find Dharma in the next life through aspiration prayers and virtue.

For Those Unable to Recognize: - Aspiration prayers for rebirth with Dharma - Recognizing signs of birth location
- Cultivating virtue to encounter teachings - The continuity of practice across lifetimes

Not Abandoning: Even those of limited capacity are not abandoned—Dharma provides methods for all.

Key Contribution: Ensures the path accommodates all beings, not just those of superior faculties.

CHAPTER 25: Spontaneous Fruition

Tibetan: རིམ་ཁང་ཉི་ཤུ་རྩ་ལྔ་པ་སྟེ་ཐ་མའོ། (Twenty-fifth Lecture Hall, the Final)
Location: Volume 2, Concluding Chapter, Sections 02-25-01-01 through 02-25-07-01
Tibetan Lines: 15635-16972
Total Liturgical Lines: ~1,337

This final chapter represents the complete revelation of fruition ('bras bu) as spontaneous display of primordial purity (ka dag) and spontaneous presence (lhun grub). Where Chapter 1 established the pure vision of the ground, this chapter demonstrates how all phenomena complete themselves in the expanse without ever departing from primordial nature.

The Great Liberation (grol sa chen po): Not attainment through effort, but the natural, self-liberated state that has been the case from the beginning. The reader arrives at recognition that nothing needed to be done, for fruition has never been separate from ground.

The Complete Dissolution: - Elements merge into expanse - Wisdom merges into expanse
- Prajñā ascends to space - Luminosity spirals in expanse - Awareness settles in its own place

Key Contribution: The bookend to Chapter 1—where beginning established pure vision, ending demonstrates that fruition was never elsewhere. Thus the Treasury is complete.
