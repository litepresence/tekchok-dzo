<classification> Passage culminates in definitive Dzogchen view: dharmata pure from beginning, without decrease, without increase, without boundary. These are definitive assertions about ground as ever-present and unchanging. Primordial purity principle establishes: nothing needs transformation because purity is innate. Risk of nihilism: readers may interpret without decrease/increase as denying conventional progress, leading to dharmakaya nihilism. Epistemic safety lies in recognizing these statements describe ground, not path—path remains necessary while ground is already perfected.

Longchenpa's definitive-provisional hermeneutics: definitive meaning is ground already perfect, but provisional meaning is path necessary for obscured beings. These are not contradictory but complementary—addressing different dimensions of teaching. Primordial purity principle does not negate need for purification but reveals purification reveals what was never impure. Similarly, accumulation is necessary conventionally but ultimately reveals nothing was ever lacking.

Essence is emptiness, nature is clarity, compassion is unobstructed—three aspects of single awareness. Longchenpa's freedom from elaborations prevents both nihilism and eternalism.
