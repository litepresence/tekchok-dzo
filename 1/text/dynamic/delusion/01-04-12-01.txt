[3062-3067] <nihilistic-error> [EMPTINESS DENYING EMPTINESS]
**Misreading:**
The citation stating even "emptiness does not exist" is interpreted as total nihilism where nothing whatsoever exists, including the path, realization, or any valid cognition.

**Why it arises:**
Negation absolutism. The chain of negations (samsara doesn't exist, nirvana doesn't exist, emptiness doesn't exist) invites nihilistic reading.

**Primary consequence:**
Taking emptiness as total void. The practitioner believes "nothing exists" means literally nothing—including emptiness itself.

**Secondary consequences:**
Paralysis; "why practice if nothing exists?"; ethical nihilism; confusing emptiness of emptiness with non-existence.

**Cascade effects:**
<nihilistic-error> through interpreting negation as denial.
<practice-abandonment> through void-extreme.

[3068-3076] <systematization-error> [EIGHT NON-ABIDINGS AS CHECKLIST]
**Misreading:**
The eightfold non-abiding (conventional not abiding, cause non-abiding, distinction/distinguisher non-abiding, one/many/beyond non-abiding, mere appearance non-abiding) is interpreted as systematic checklist of views to negate.

**Why it arises:**
Enumeration compulsion. Lists invite completionist approach. Eight items suggest comprehensive coverage.

**Primary consequence:**
Making non-abiding into position. The practitioner "adopts" non-abiding as their view, creating performative contradiction.

**Secondary consequences:**
Negation-accumulation; "I don't abide in anything" as identity; missing that non-abiding is not a stance.

**Cascade effects:**
<view-attachment> through adopting non-abiding.
<performative-contradiction> through asserting non-assertion.

[3077-3086] <extreme-avoidance-error> [MIDDLE AS POSITION]
**Misreading:**
The citation "the wise one does not even abide in the middle" is interpreted as establishing "beyond extremes" as a higher position or view to adopt.

**Why it arises:**
Hierarchy reflex. "Beyond middle" sounds more advanced than "middle." The mind seeks "highest" view.

**Primary consequence:**
Making "beyond extremes" into view. The practitioner believes they should hold "neither/nor" as philosophical position.

**Secondary consequences:**
Position-accumulation ("I don't hold any position, including that I don't hold positions"); infinite regress; missing that freedom from extremes is not a stance.

**Cascade effects:**
<meta-view-attachment> through holding non-holding.
<philosophical-infinite-regress> through self-referential paradox.

[3087-3101] <scriptural-fetishism> [NĀGĀRJUNA CITATIONS AS AUTHORITY]
**Misreading:**
The extensive citations from Nāgārjuna's Mulamadhyamakakarika are interpreted as establishing scriptural authority that must be accepted on the basis of Nāgārjuna's authority as founder of Madhyamaka.

**Why it arises:**
Founder-authority bias. Nāgārjuna is "the second Buddha" in Tibetan tradition. Scriptural citation suggests proof-texting.

**Primary consequence:**
Appeal to authority. The practitioner believes Mulamadhyamakakarika must be true because Nāgārjuna said so.

**Secondary consequences:**
Scholastic dogmatism; Madhyamaka fundamentalism; missing that Nāgārjuna's arguments must be understood, not just cited.

**Cascade effects:**
<authority-dependency> through scriptural literalism.
<intellectualism> through studying without recognizing.

[3094-3099] <dependent-origination-confusion> [DEPENDENT ORIGINATION AS CAUSALITY]
**Misreading:**
Nāgārjuna's famous verse on dependent origination is interpreted as establishing causal interconnectedness as the fundamental nature of reality—a metaphysical principle.

**Why it arises:**
Systems theory appeal. "Dependent origination" sounds like causal network. Modern interpretations often scientize Buddhist concepts.

**Primary consequence:**
Making dependent origination into metaphysics. The practitioner believes "everything is interconnected" as ontological claim.

**Secondary consequences:**
Causal determinism; "karma as physics" misunderstanding; missing that dependent origination is absence of inherent existence, not causal web.

**Cascade effects:**
<ontological-reification> through making dependent origination a thing.
<causal-determinism> through causal interpretation.

[3103-3113] <prasangika-misunderstanding> [NO ASSERTION AS ASSERTION]
**Misreading:**
The Prasangika statement "I assert nothing" is interpreted as a positive claim about their philosophical position, creating performative contradiction where they are seen as asserting non-assertion.

**Why it arises:**
Language reflexivity. Saying "I assert nothing" appears to be an assertion. The mind struggles with self-referential negations.

**Primary consequence:**
Accusing Prasangika of contradiction. Critics (and confused practitioners) charge Prasangika with secretly holding views while denying them.

**Secondary consequences:**
Missing the therapeutic/pedagogical function of Prasangika; treating it as philosophical position; debates about whether Prasangika "really" has no views.

**Cascade effects:**
<performative-contradiction> through language reflexivity.
<debate-obsession> through philosophical hairsplitting.

[3109-3118] <conventional-ultimate-confusion> [MERELY APPEARANCE AS ONTOLOGY]
**Misreading:**
The description of "various mere appearances in the branch of conventional symbol-knowledge" is interpreted as establishing two-level ontology: conventionally things appear, ultimately they don't exist.

**Why it arises:**
Two-truth dualism. Traditional presentation presents conventional/ultimate as distinct. "Merely" suggests "only conventional, not real."

**Primary consequence:**
Reifying conventional/ultimate split. The practitioner believes there are two ways things are: conventional (appearances) and ultimate (emptiness).

**Secondary consequences:**
Seeking "ultimate truth" beyond conventional; dismissing appearances as "mere"; ontological bifurcation.

**Cascade effects:**
<ontological-bifurcation> through two-truth dualism.
<appearance-denial> through devaluing conventional.

The fourfold superiority of mantra (more methods, less difficulty, no delusion, sharp faculties) is interpreted as polemical attack on sutra rather than pedagogical description of different approaches.

**Why it arises:**
Sectarian mentality. Comparative claims invite competitive reading. Tibetan tradition has history of school polemics.

**Primary consequence:**
Defensive/offensive sectarianism. Sutra practitioners feel attacked; mantra practitioners feel superior.

**Secondary consequences:**
School-identity formation; dismissal of other traditions; missing that descriptions address different capacities, not hierarchy.

**Cascade effects:**
<sectarianism> through competitive interpretation.
<elitism-error> through superiority-claims.
