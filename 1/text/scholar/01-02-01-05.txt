# Fourth Tier: The Four Great Kings (Lokapālas)
**Analysis:** This section completes the four-tier structure of the Heaven of the Thirty-Three by introducing the fourth and final tier (*bang rim bzhi pa*). Following the pattern of protective deities (first tier), flower-garland bearers (second tier), and nectar-intoxicated gods (third tier), this fourth tier presents the directional guardians.

**The Fivefold Structure:**
1. **Place** (*gnas*): Fourth tier in the four directions
2. **Beings** (*'gro ba*): Elevated-born (*'phags skye po*) - the Four Great Kings
3. **Attributes**: Each king with specific iconographic marks
4. **Retinue** (*'khor*): Individual retinues dwelling together
5. **Function**: Protection of the cosmic order

**Architectural Logic:** The progression from coarse (protection) through refined (aesthetic) to subtle (intoxication) culminates here in sovereign guardianship. The directional placement (four cardinal directions) establishes the spatial coordinates of the divine realm.

## The Four Kings: Terminological Analysis
**Analysis:**

The text presents three of the Four Great Kings (*rgyal chen bzhi*):

1. **Snyan mi bzang** (*spyan mi bzang*) — Virūpākṣa
   - "Ugly-eyes" or "Disfigured-eye"
   - Guardian of the West
   - Genitive construction: *spyan* (eyes) + *mi bzang* (not beautiful)

2. **Rnam thos kyi bu** (*rnam thos kyi bu*) — Vaiśravaṇa/Kuvera
   - "Son of He-who-hears-all"
   - Guardian of the North
   - The one who hears the sounds/cries of the world

3. **Yul 'khor srung** (*yul 'khor srung*) — Dhṛtarāṣṭra
   - "Land/Realm Guardian"
   - Guardian of the East
   - Compound: *yul* (land) + *'khor* (circle/realm) + *srung* (guard)

**Missing Fourth:** Virūḍhaka (*'phags skye po* — "Growing/Elevated-born"), guardian of the South, is implied but not explicitly named in this enumeration.

**Key Particles:**
- *Dang* (lines 999-1002): Enumerative particle connecting the kings
- *La* (line 999): Locative marking the tier
- *Kyi* (line 1001): Genitive-connective in compound names
- *Dang bcas pa* (line 1002): "Together with" - indicating retinue

## The Four Great Kings in Buddhist Cosmology
**Analysis:** The *rgyal chen bzhi* (Four Great Kings) are central figures in Buddhist cosmology, adapted from Indian guardian deities (Lokapālas). In Nyingma tradition, they serve multiple functions:

**Cosmological Role:**
- Guardians of the four cardinal directions
- Protectors of the Dharma from external threats
- Cosmic axis establishing space

**Abhidharma Classification:**
- Class: Desire-realm gods (*'dod khams kyi lha*)
- Location: Lower slopes of Mount Sumeru
- Function: Protection and enforcement of cosmic order

**Tantric Integration:**
In Nyingma practice, these kings appear in:
- *Kangso* (propitiation) rituals
- *Bsrung 'khor* (protective circles)
- Maṇḍala architecture as directional guardians

**Scriptural Sources:**
- *Abhidharmakośa* (Vasubandhu): Detailed cosmological descriptions
- *Divyāvadāna*: Mythological narratives
- *Mahāvastu*: Jātaka-style stories

**Dzogchen Perspective:**
From the Great Perfection view, these "kings" are spontaneous displays (*lhun grub*) of primordial wisdom, appearing to protect practitioners but ultimately empty of inherent existence.

## The Four Groves as Maṇḍala Architecture
**Analysis:** The four groves create a maṇḍala structure:

**Cardinal Directions:**
- East: Chariots (movement, dawn)
- South: Violence/Change (power, protection)
- West: Enjoyment (completion, fulfillment)
- North: Joy (beyond sorrow, liberation)

**Buddhist Adaptation:**
Originally Indic cosmology, Buddhist-ized through:
- Removal of violence (even the "violent" grove serves pleasure)
- Focus on enjoyment (not power)
- Ultimate impermanence (even this joy ends)

**Maṇḍala Correspondences:**
- Center: Śakra/Indra (east is usually foremost, but here center)
- Four directions: Four activities (*phrin las bzhi*)
  - East: Pacifying
  - South: Increasing
  - West: Magnetizing
  - North: Wrathful

**Dzogchen View:**
These groves represent the four "immeasurables" (*tshad med bzhi*):
- East: Equanimity (chariots move freely)
- South: Compassion (protection from harm)
- West: Love (enjoyment)
- North: Joy (freedom from sorrow)

**Ritual Function:**
In Nyingma practice, one visualizes these realms as the pure land where practice occurs—transformation of ordinary perception into divine display.

## The Four Groves as Cognitive Modes
**Analysis:** From a philosophical perspective, the four groves represent four modes of divine consciousness:

**East (Chariots):** *Gzugs* (Form)
- Visual perception
- Movement and variety
- The manifold world of form

**South (Violence/Change):** *Tshor ba* (Feeling)
- Sensation, pleasant and painful
- Change and transformation
- Reactivity to experience

**West (Enjoyment):** *Du shes* (Perception)
- Recognition and enjoyment
- Conceptual engagement
- Mixed perceptions

**North (Joy): *'Du byed* (Formations)

- Universal joy (all-encompassing)
- Liberation from limitations

**Beyond the Four:**
Even these perfect divine groves remain within the fifth aggregate—*rnam shes* (consciousness)—the container within which all experience occurs.

**Dzogchen Synthesis:**
The four groves ultimately resolve into the "ground grove" (*gzhi'i tshal*)—the nature of mind itself, beyond direction, containing all directions, the source from which these four spontaneously arise.
