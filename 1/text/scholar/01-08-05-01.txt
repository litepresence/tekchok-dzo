Essence-Nature as Non-Dual Ground
The section presents a sophisticated metaphysical framework where ground (gzhi) possesses two aspects that are nevertheless non-dual (dbyer med). Essence (ngo bo, svabhāva) is characterized as primordially pure (ka dag)—free from all adventitious defilements from the beginning. Nature (rang bzhin, prakṛti) is characterized as spontaneously present (lhun grub)—manifesting all phenomenal variety without effort. The non-duality lies in the fact that essence is not separate from nature; the purity does not negate appearance, nor does appearance compromise purity.

The Eight Grounds Controversy
The objection that ground would absurdly be eightfold (gzhi brgyad) references a specific doxographic controversy in Dzogchen metaphysics. Longchenpa's system typically presents seven grounds (gzhi bdun), including: dharmakāya ground, radiance ground, vajra chain ground, etc. The potential eighth ground would arise from treating ka dag and lhun grub as separate grounds rather than aspects of a single ground. The response clarifies that Longchenpa asserts their non-duality (dbyer med), maintaining the sevenfold schema without addition.

[10774-10774] <concept>
Non-Duality Preserves Enumeration
The philosophical subtlety here concerns how asserting the non-duality of primordial purity and spontaneous presence preserves the sevenfold ground structure. If ka dag and lhun grub were separate grounds, the enumeration would expand to eight. But since they are dual aspects (rnam pa gnyis) of a single ground—essence and nature—the sevenfold structure remains intact. This exemplifies the Mādhyamika principle that non-duality does not obliterate conventional distinctions.
