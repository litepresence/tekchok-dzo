**Analysis:** This minimal structural marker presents the first (*dang po*) of a sequential enumeration, identifying "recognition" (*ngos zin*) as the initial stage. The structure uses the locative particle *la* to mark this as a stage or station. The absence of additional content suggests this is a transitional enumeration marker within a larger presentation of progressive stages. The formula "First: there is X" (*dang po la X*) indicates the beginning of a numbered sequence, likely the four stages of recognition, realization, dissolving, or similar progression.

[14588] <philology>
**Analysis:** *dang po la ngos zin*—"First: there is recognition." The construction employs the ordinal *dang po* (first) with the locative particle *la*, which serves multiple functions here: (1) indicating the "stage" or "point" at which recognition occurs; (2) marking the topical division; (3) suggesting continuation—"as for the first [stage], there is..." The term *ngos zin* (recognition/identified) combines *ngos* (face/aspect) with *zin* (seized/obtained)—the technical term for recognizing the nature of mind. The absence of case markers on *ngos zin* suggests an existential construction—"recognition exists/is present."

[14588] <context>
**Analysis:** This marker appears within Chapter 23 on Fruition (*'bras bu*), likely as part of the presentation of progressive stages in the completion of practice. The term *ngos zin* (recognition) in Dzogchen contexts typically refers to the initial *ngos 'dzin* (pointing-out and recognition) of awareness (*rig pa*), but here in the fruition chapter, it likely indicates a more advanced recognition—perhaps the recognition of exhaustion (*zad pa*) or the recognition that all phenomena are awareness's display. Common fourfold sequences in Dzogchen fruition include: (1) recognition (*ngos 'dzin*); (2) realization (*rtogs pa*); (3) dissolving (*thim pa*); (4) liberation (*grol ba*). Alternatively, this may mark stages of the *'bras bu* (fruition) itself: recognition of the basis, realization of the path, manifestation of the result.

[14588] <concept>
**Analysis:** The placement of "recognition" as the first stage in a fruition chapter emphasizes the Dzogchen principle that recognition is the foundation of all subsequent attainment. Unlike gradualist paths where recognition comes after purification, in Dzogchen recognition is primary—*gdod ma nas dag pa* (primordially pure) means recognition reveals what was always already the case. The term *ngos zin* specifically indicates decisive recognition (*nges pa rtogs pa*) rather than mere intellectual understanding (*go ba*). At the fruition stage, this recognition has deepened from initial pointing-out to effortless natural recognition (*rjen pa'i ngos 'dzin*)—awareness continuously recognizes itself without deliberate application. This first stage likely corresponds to the recognition that appearances are exhausted (*snang ba zad pa*), fixation is exhausted (*'dzin pa zad pa*), and only the natural state remains.
