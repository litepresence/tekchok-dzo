. Pure vision: seeing phenomena as deity display.

[3709-3719] <philology>
The term dag snang (pure vision) carries its technical Dzogchen sense of recognizing appearances as sacred display rather than ordinary impure perception. Instrumental particles (-kyis, -gis) indicate the means by which perception is transformed. The causative construction sgom pa (cultivation) establishes that pure vision is developed through practice before becoming spontaneous recognition.

[3720-3734] <context>
Citations from Thal 'gyur, Rang shar, and Nyi zla kha sbyor establish doctrinal authority. The zhes so formula indicates transmission lineage within bka' ma tradition. Pure vision represents the tantric foundation that Dzogchen presupposes—seeing all phenomena as the play of deity, mantra, and wisdom.

[3735-3751] <structure>
The passage demonstrates systematic presentation. Structural markers rim pa organize pure vision practice: first recognizing one's own body as deity, then speech as mantra, then mind as wisdom, finally all appearances as the deity's mandala. The dbye ba distinguishes pure vision from ordinary impure perception.

[3752-3762] <concept>
The operational logic—cause (ordinary impure perception), method (pure vision cultivation), result (spontaneous sacred perception)—integrates tantric practice within Great Perfection. Pure vision becomes effortless when recognized as the natural display of awareness.
