[1902-1905] <view-collapse> <categorical-fixation>
**Misreading:**
The classification of "erroneous vehicles" (*log pa'i theg pa*) interpreted as establishing a binary division between correct Buddhism and incorrect non-Buddhism, creating an us/them dichotomy.

**Why it arises:**
Polemical literature invites partisan reading. The term "log pa" (erroneous) suggests complete falsehood. Defensive religious identity seeks clear boundaries.

**Primary consequence:**
Dzogchen becomes sectarian exclusionism. Practitioners dismiss all non-Buddhist wisdom while failing to recognize their own subtle reifications.

**Secondary consequences:**
Interfaith dialogue is rejected. Comparative philosophy is seen as dangerous. The universality of direct recognition is obscured by institutional boundaries.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <sectarian-elitism> (Buddhist superiority), <boundary-policing> (who is in/out), <wisdom-denial> (rejecting valid insights), <institutional-identity> (affiliation over recognition).

[1906-1910] <numerical-literalism> <taxonomy-obsession>
**Misreading:**
The "360 views" (*sum brgya drug cu*) interpreted as a precise quantitative count of wrong positions to be catalogued and memorized.

**Why it arises:**
Specific numbers suggest comprehensiveness. Academic tendencies want complete taxonomies. The enumeration invites list-making.

**Primary consequence:**
Doxography becomes intellectual accumulation. Practitioners study views to catalog errors rather than recognize how their own mind fixates.

**Secondary consequences:**
Scholastic pride in knowing all 360 views. The living recognition of fixation patterns is replaced by academic classification.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <catalog-compulsion> (collecting views), <intellectual-pride> (knowing all errors), <scholastic-obsession> (study over practice), <enumerative-fetishism> (counting wrong views).

[1911-1915] <substantialist-self> <atman-projection>
**Misreading:**
The Samkhya "self" (*bdag*) described as thumb-sized, proportionate to body, existing in hearts—interpreted as confirming Hindu atman doctrine, allowing practitioners to dismiss it entirely without examining their own subtle self-grasping.

**Why it arises:**
Samkhya sounds like Vedanta. The physical description seems naive. Buddhist critique of atman appears to invalidate the whole view.

**Primary consequence:**
Practitioners fail to recognize that Samkhya describes genuine meditative experience (accessing subtle mind/ālaya) but reifies it as permanent self. This leads to either dismissing valid experience or fearing any experience of continuity.

**Secondary consequences:**
The distinction between coarse and subtle self-grasping is lost. Practitioners believe they've transcended self-view while maintaining subtle identification with awareness.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <experience-denial> (fearing continuity), <subtle-atman> (identifying with awareness), <meditative-avoidance> (fearing samadhi), <reification-blindness> (missing own fixation).

[1916-1923] <size-reification> <spatial-self>
**Misreading:**
The self's varying sizes (thumb, pea, sesame) interpreted as literal anatomical claims about the soul's dimensions—physicalist reduction of spiritual concepts.

**Why it arises:**
Concrete measurements invite literalization. The description resembles vitalist theories. "Size of self" suggests spatial entity.

**Primary consequence:**
Samkhya is dismissed as primitive materialism. The metaphorical function (subtle presence proportionate to embodiment) is missed.

**Secondary consequences:**
Practitioners develop materialist superiority. The sophisticated phenomenology (subtle body as experiential domain) is reduced to "they think soul has size."

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <materialist-arrogance> (scientific superiority), <metaphor-blindness> (missing symbolism), <phenomenological-dismissal> (rejecting subtle body), <size-obsession> (measuring awareness).

[1924-1929] <three-poisons-equipoise> <affective-substantialism>
**Misreading:**
The three poisons (desire, hatred, delusion) as constituent elements in equipoise interpreted as confirming that emotions are fundamental substances—emotional essentialism.

**Why it arises:**
"Constituents" suggests building blocks. The equipoise metaphor implies substantial elements. Western psychology debates emotion vs. cognition primacy.

**Primary consequence:**
Afflictions are substantialized into permanent features of mind. Practitioners believe desire/hatred are unavoidable essence rather than adventitious confusion.

**Secondary consequences:**
Moral struggle against essentialized emotions. "I am just angry by nature" becomes justification. Purification is abandoned as impossible.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <essentialist-emotion> (afflictions as nature), <moral-fatalism> (cannot change), <purification-avoidance> (why bother), <emotional-determinism> (ruled by passions).

[1930-1935] <self-cognition-mysticism> <reflexive-reification>
**Misreading:**
"The self knows its own nature through self-cognition" (*rang gis rig*) interpreted as describing genuine reflexive awareness—confirmation of self-illuminating consciousness.

**Why it arises:**
Self-cognition is debated in philosophy of mind. Dzogchen also speaks of self-recognition (*rang ngo shes*). The terminology overlaps.

**Primary consequence:**
Practitioners conflate Samkhya self-cognition with Dzogchen self-recognition. The reified self-knowing of Samkhya is mistaken for Dzogchen rigpa.

**Secondary consequences:**
Dzogchen becomes Samkhya in Buddhist guise. The permanent self is smuggled in as "awareness." Non-dual recognition is replaced by self-knowing entity.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <rigpa-reification> (awareness as self), <atman-dzogchen> (Hindu-Buddhist confusion), <reflexive-fixation> (self-knowing as goal), <recognition-misunderstanding> (missing emptiness).

[1936-1941] <linguistic-divergence> <schismatic-essentialism>
**Misreading:**
The branching into sub-schools based on "subtle distinctions" interpreted as showing that philosophical differences are merely verbal or sectarian rather than reflecting genuine phenomenological variations.

**Why it arises:**
"Subtle distinctions" sounds like hair-splitting. Multiple schools seem unnecessary. Ecumenical impulses favor unity over diversity.

**Primary consequence:**
Genuine phenomenological differences are dismissed as sectarianism. Practitioners adopt "all religions are the same" without understanding distinctions.

**Secondary consequences:**
Important nuances are flattened. The specific insights of each view are lost. Dzogchen becomes generic mysticism.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <flattened-ecumenism> (all same), <nuance-blindness> (missing differences), <phenomenological-dismissal> (rejecting precision), <syncretic-confusion> (mixing views).

[1942-1948] <nihilism-reduction> <annihilation-misunderstanding>
**Misreading:**
Lokayata/Charvaka nihilism (*chad par lta ba*) interpreted as simple denial of everything—valueless hedonism that negates meaning and morality.

**Why it arises:**
"Nihilism" in English suggests despair and meaninglessness. The denial of rebirth appears to justify hedonism. "Only this life" sounds like carpe diem.

**Primary consequence:**
Practitioners fail to see that Lokayata accurately perceives the impermanence of gross phenomena but misses subtle continuity. This leads to either nihilistic despair or reactive eternalism.

**Secondary consequences:**
The partial validity of Lokayata (materialism as antidote to spiritual materialism) is missed. Practitioners swing between materialism and spiritualism.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <nihilistic-despair> (nothing matters), <reactive-eternalism> (fleeing to permanence), <materialist-dismissal> (rejecting insights), <meaning-obsession> (seeking significance).

[1949-1957] <causal-nihilism-confusion> <emergent-consciousness>
**Misreading:**
Causal nihilism's claim that consciousness emerges from elements interpreted as primitive emergentism—materialism dressed in spiritual language.

**Why it arises:**
"Consciousness from elements" resembles modern materialism. The claim sounds scientific. Emergentism is debated in philosophy of mind.

**Primary consequence:**
The sophisticated phenomenology (subtle consciousness latent in matter) is reduced to "they think brain creates mind." Important distinctions are lost.

**Secondary consequences:**
Practitioners dismiss genuine insights about consciousness-world relationship. The reification of consciousness AS elements is missed.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <materialist-projection> (modern scientism), <emergentism-dismissal> (rejecting valid insights), <phenomenological-reduction> (missing subtlety), <consciousness-mystery> (unsolved problem).

[1958-1969] <panpsychism-attraction> <universal-consciousness>
**Misreading:**
The claim that all objects (wood, stones, crops) possess consciousness interpreted as confirming panpsychism—universal mind as appealing alternative to materialism.

**Why it arises:**
Panpsychism is popular in philosophy of mind. "Everything is conscious" seems profound. Rejection of materialism appeals to spiritual practitioners.

**Primary consequence:**
Lokayata is appropriated as proto-Dzogchen. The fundamental reification (consciousness as substantial entity) is adopted rather than recognized.

**Secondary consequences:**
Practitioners develop nature-mysticism based on reified consciousness. Trees and rocks are personified. The emptiness of all phenomena is missed.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <panpsychist-appropriation> (everything conscious), <nature-mysticism> (rocks have feelings), <environmental-reification> (conscious earth), <spiritual-materialism> (things possess mind).

---

[1970-1984] <adventitious-emergence> <aggregation-mysticism>
**Misreading:**
The adventitious aggregation metaphor (*glo bur du 'dus pa*) interpreted as confirming Buddhist dependent origination—Lokayata as accidental Buddhist.

**Why it arises:**
"Adventitious" and "aggregation" sound like Buddhist terms. The spontaneous arising resembles dependent origination. Common ground is sought.

**Primary consequence:**
Important differences are obscured. Lokayata's radical momentariness is conflated with Buddhist dependent origination. The nihilistic extreme is missed.

**Secondary consequences:**
Practitioners believe Lokayata is "close to Buddhism." The genuine critique (Lokayata denies continuity that Buddhism affirms) is lost.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <conflation-error> (merging distinct views), <common-ground-fetishism> (seeking unity), <boundary-blur> (losing distinctions), <appropriation-misunderstanding> (claiming others' views).

---

[1985-2001] <resultant-nihilism> <annihilation-terror>
**Misreading:**
Resultant nihilism's denial of post-death existence interpreted as describing genuine annihilation—total cessation that must be feared and avoided.

**Why it arises:**
Fear of death is universal. Annihilation sounds like worst outcome. Buddhist teachings on impermanence seem to affirm continuity.

**Primary consequence:**
Annihilation is substantialized as terrifying void. Practitioners develop death anxiety or reactive pursuit of permanence. The emptiness of cessation is missed.

**Secondary consequences:**
Fear motivates spiritual practice. Liberation is sought to avoid annihilation. The peace of cessation is rejected as nihilistic.

**Cascade effects:**
Triggers <death-anxiety> (fearing cessation), <eternalistic-flight> (fleeing to permanence), <liberation-fear> (avoiding peace), <existential-terror> (annihilation dread).
