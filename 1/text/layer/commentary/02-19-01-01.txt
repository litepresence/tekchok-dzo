Before recognition dawned... spent three years arguing with scholars about whether mind has a cause.
They brought scriptures, logic, citations from India and Tibet. Finally, exhausted, he sat
by a frozen stream and watched ice form. The ice did not ask permission to freeze. The
water did not seek a cause to flow. Mind, he realized, is like the stream—appearing to
move, never leaving its nature.

[6414-6574]
The text continues with extensive practical instructions for integrating recognition into all activities: eating, sleeping, walking, working, relating. Each activity becomes an opportunity for recognition. Each moment becomes the ground of liberation. This is not future attainment. This is present practice.

"Washing rice, mind has no cause.
Cooking rice, awareness naturally present.
Eating rice, recognition complete.
What need for temple? What need for cave?
This kitchen is the pure land.
This spoon is the vajra.
This rice is the offering.
This eating is the practice."

[7575-8082]
The final section of this vast teaching returns to the beginning: mind has no cause, no conditions, no arising, no ceasing. All the elaborate analysis, all the detailed instructions, all the scriptural citations point to this simple recognition. What was never produced cannot be destroyed. What was never bound cannot be liberated. What was never confused cannot be enlightened.
