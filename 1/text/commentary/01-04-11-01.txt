[3040-3052] The first Svātantrika position: objects and perceivers aren't established, but non-established awareness-wisdom, inexpressible in words or thought, exists ultimately in the manner of illusion. The qualities of meditation—the paths and bhūmis—and the final result, the Buddha's illusion-like wisdom, having attained that, one acts for beings from within the dharmakāya. From the Mother Sūtra: "All phenomena are like a dream, like an illusion. Even nirvāṇa is like a dream, like an illusion. If there were any phenomenon higher than nirvāṇa, that too would be like a dream, like an illusion." All phenomena are the various objects and consciousnesses. Nirvāṇa means manifesting the form kāya and acting for beings. Therefore, there is no phenomenon higher than the self-cognizing suchness and the sublime dharmakāya—this is their position.

[3053-3061] Among these, some assert illusion as truly established, some as merely appearing like illusion, some as simply illusion—many variations. These are called the lower Svātantrikas. The second position says that ultimately not even illusion is established. Without objects or aspects, even self-cognizing experience of their absence doesn't exist—like without a daughter there's no consciousness apprehending her.
