This section continues the refutation of non-Buddhist views, focusing on the nihilist position (chad par lta ba) represented primarily by the Lokayata (rgyang 'phan pa) or Carvaka school. This materialist philosophy denies the existence of previous and future lives, the efficacy of karma, and the possibility of liberation, asserting that consciousness arises spontaneously from the elements without cause. The [NIHILISTIC ERROR] lies in denying continuity and causation, falling into the extreme of nihilism.

The epistemic stratification [DZOGCHEN VIEW] vs [WRONG VIEW] demonstrates how Dzogchen avoids both eternalism and nihilism. While refuting the nihilist denial of karma and rebirth, Dzogchen also recognizes that these phenomena are empty of inherent existence, neither truly existent nor truly non-existent. The middle way transcends both positions without affirming either.

The text presents two types of nihilism: cause-nihilism (rgyu chad par smra ba) denying the continuity of mind from life to life, and result-nihilism ('bras bu chad par smra ba) denying the effects of actions. The Carvaka position holds that mind exists latently in the four elements, emerging when conditions of moisture and heat combine, analogous to how intoxication arises from fermentation. This [ONTOLOGICAL ERROR] reduces consciousness to a material process, failing to recognize its empty yet luminous nature.

From the [ULTIMATE TRUTH] perspective, neither the nihilist denial nor the eternalist affirmation captures the nature of reality. Dependent origination (rten 'brel) demonstrates that phenomena arise through causes and conditions without inherent existence, avoiding both extremes. The [DZOGCHEN VIEW] recognizes rig pa as the ground from which all appearances arise, yet this ground is itself empty and ungraspable.

The viewpoint tag [PROVISIONAL MEANING] applies to the entire refutation, which functions as [UPAYA] to protect beings from falling into wrong views. The section uses [VIEW ASSERTION] to clearly delineate what constitutes correct versus incorrect understanding, establishing the boundaries of authentic dharma.

The refutation employs standard Buddhist arguments against spontaneous arising (ngo bo nyid kyis 'byung ba), using the seed-sprout analogy to demonstrate the necessity of cause and effect. While these arguments operate at the level of conventional truth (kun rdzob bden pa), they prepare the ground for the [DEFINITIVE MEANING] recognition of emptiness.

The epistemic stance maintains that wrong views are not merely intellectual errors but obstacles to liberation. By denying karma and rebirth, the nihilist view removes the foundation for ethical action and spiritual progress. Dzogchen's transcendence of both eternalism and nihilism thus has profound practical implications, grounding ethical conduct in the recognition of dependent origination while recognizing its empty nature.

The section concludes the presentation of non-Buddhist views, having established through refutation of both extremes the necessity of the middle way, which forms the philosophical foundation for the tantric and Dzogchen vehicles that follow.
