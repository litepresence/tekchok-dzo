This section presents Upa tantra (spyod rgyud), the second of the three outer tantras, representing a crucial bridge between the dualistic approach of Kriya and the internalized approach of Yoga tantra. Upa balances external ritual with internal meditation, integrating the method of deity yoga with the wisdom of emptiness. From the Dzogchen perspective, Upa introduces the non-dual view that will be fully realized in higher tantras.

The epistemic framework here employs both [PROVISIONAL MEANING] and elements of [DEFINITIVE MEANING]. Upa's view recognizes the deity and practitioner as ultimately non-dual while maintaining conventional distinctions for practice. This integration of method (upaya, thabs) and wisdom (prajna, shes rab) foreshadows the inner tantras' approach.

The [DZOGCHEN VIEW] recognizes Upa as a transitional vehicle that introduces key concepts without fully realizing them. The non-duality of deity and practitioner, the integration of external ritual with internal realization, and the balance of activity with meditation all point toward Dzogchen's recognition, yet remain conceptual rather than direct.

The distinction between [ULTIMATE TRUTH] and [CONVENTIONAL TRUTH] operates in Upa as an integrated perspective rather than a hierarchical one. Conventionally, the deity is worshipped through ritual; ultimately, deity and practitioner are one in emptiness. The practice involves both aspects simultaneously, neither abandoning ritual for meditation nor meditation for ritual.

The viewpoint tag [VIEW ASSERTION] establishes Upa's specific perspective: the deity is both external and internal, both same and different from the practitioner; ritual and meditation are equally important; results come through the integration of method and wisdom. This view is appropriate for practitioners of intermediate capacity.

The [MEDITATION INSTRUCTION] component describes Upa's distinctive practices: deity yoga that balances external visualization with internal absorption, mantra recitation integrated with insight meditation, and ritual activity performed with awareness of emptiness. The deity may be visualized in front or within the heart center, reflecting the balance of external and internal.

The section emphasizes Upa's focus on conduct (spyod pa), particularly the integration of tantric practice with daily activities. Unlike Kriya's emphasis on ritual purity separate from ordinary life, Upa teaches how to transform all activities into the path. Eating, sleeping, and moving become occasions for practice.

The epistemic stance maintains [DRANG DON] with elements of [NGES DON], recognizing Upa's transitional nature. It functions as a bridge between the dualistic framework of Kriya and the internalized approach of Yoga, preparing practitioners for the more subtle methods of inner tantra.

The section thus presents Upa as the synthesis of external and internal approaches, introducing the key principle of non-duality that will be developed fully in subsequent vehicles. The practitioner learns to hold view and conduct simultaneously, neither abandoning the world for meditation nor meditation for the world.
