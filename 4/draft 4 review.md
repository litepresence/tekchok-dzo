## 1. Global Assessment

**This is not a casual or amateur translation.**
It is already far beyond what most English Dzogchen renderings attempt.

However:

> **It does *not yet* meet Khenpo-standard.**

The reason is *not* doctrinal misunderstanding, nor lack of intimacy with dgongs pa. The issue is subtler and more dangerous:

**The commentary too often performs recognition instead of transmitting it.**

From a Tibetan khenpo’s perspective, this is the single most serious fault remaining.

A khenpo will not object to boldness, nor to collapsing language, nor to directness. But they will object when:

* commentary **replaces** the text’s work instead of **opening space for it**
* recognition is **staged rhetorically** rather than **left bare**
* the translator’s insight becomes **too continuously present**

This draft often walks right up to the edge—and occasionally crosses it.

## 3. Critical Failures & Risks (This is where Khenpos will object)

### 1. Commentary Voice: **Too continuously “awake”**

Your own standard says:

> *Permit asymmetry. Let some statements stand without immediate collapse.*

Yet many commentaries **collapse every paragraph**, often in the final sentence, sometimes twice.

Example pattern (repeated frequently):

* establish an image
* clarify it
* immediately dissolve subject/object
* then explicitly instruct the reader to “notice”

This creates **didactic saturation**.

A khenpo expects:

* some commentaries to **end unresolved**
* some to end with **image only**
* some to end with **no collapse at all**

Right now, the reader is rarely left alone long enough.

---

### 2. Subtle AI-signature remains—not in structure, but in *density*

You successfully avoided triplet constructions and mechanical parallelism.

However, another AI-signature remains:

> **Uniform intensity of insight per paragraph**

Human cognition—even realized cognition—does not land recognition with the same force every time. Tibetan commentarial prose often includes:

* “ordinary” sentences
* descriptive transitions
* moments of restraint

Your commentary is **almost always at peak voltage**.

This paradoxically weakens transmission.

---

### 3. Translator Presence is occasionally too explicit

Phrases like:

* “Notice this now”
* “Try it now”
* “As you read”

are pedagogically effective—but **overused**.

A khenpo will ask:

> “Why is the translator standing between the reader and the text?”

The strongest Tibetan commentaries *disappear* just before recognition.

You need **more vanishing points**.

---

### 4. Some collapses are described rather than enacted

Your own criterion:

> *Never describe the mechanism of collapse. Enact it.*

Most of the time, you succeed.

But occasionally you say things like:

* “this collapses seeker and sought”
* “distance dissolves”
* “collapse of separation”

These are **meta-descriptions**, not enactments.

A khenpo will mark these as instructional residue.

---

### 5. Warnings and disclosures are doctrinally correct—but rhetorically Western

The **AI Methodology Disclosure** and **Unauthorized Translation Disclosure** are ethically commendable.

However, from a Tibetan point of view:

* they are **too explicit**
* too apologetic
* too self-referential

A khenpo would never object to humility—but they would prefer:

* fewer explanations
* more silence
* trust in reader discernment

This is not a doctrinal problem, but a **cultural register mismatch**.

---

## 4. Exact Steps Required for Final Khenpo-Standard Refinement

### STEP 1: Institute a “Silence Ratio” for Commentary

For every **3 commentaries**:

* 1 may fully collapse
* 1 should stop at image or function
* 1 should end *without resolution*

Do this deliberately.

Ask:

> “What happens if I remove the final sentence?”

Often: transmission improves.

---

### STEP 2: Remove ~20–30% of explicit reader-address

Specifically reduce:

* “notice”
* “now”
* “as you read”
* “try it”

Not eliminate—**thin**.

Let the text do more work.

---

### STEP 3: Allow some commentaries to be *pedestrian*

This is counterintuitive but essential.

Some paragraphs should feel:

* descriptive
* almost boring
* merely clarifying syntax or imagery

Khenpos trust dgongs pa to work **without constant ignition**.

---

### STEP 4: Replace collapse-descriptions with grammatical enactment

Whenever you say:

* “this collapses…”
* “distance dissolves…”
* “subject and object…”

Rewrite so the **sentence itself does the work**.

If you cannot do this, **cut the sentence**.

---

### STEP 5: Thin the Translator’s Authority Signal

You do not need to assert:

* discernment
* responsibility
* sovereignty

Your work already demonstrates it.

Consider compressing the disclosure sections by **half**, keeping only what is strictly necessary.

---

### STEP 6: Final Khenpo Test (Non-negotiable)

Before finalizing each stanza:

1. Read only the Tibetan aloud
2. Read only the liturgical English aloud
3. Read the commentary **silently**, once
4. Remove any sentence that still feels helpful

If transmission weakens after removal, restore it.
If transmission strengthens, the sentence was egoic—even subtly.

---

## Final Verdict

This draft is **very close**.

It already exceeds most published English Dzogchen translations in:

* fidelity
* courage
* intimacy with dgongs pa

What remains is **not more insight**, but **more restraint**.

Khenpo-standard is reached not when nothing more can be added,
but when nothing more is needed.
